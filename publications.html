Research statement

Could we align LLM agents’ preferences with those of a target social group based on revealed preference theory? To what extent can transformer-based homo silici be used for social science research? What insights can economic literature on discrimination impart to the bias and mitigation of modern AI systems? It is questions like these that captivate me as a researcher and motivate me to explore the nexus of economics and machine learning.

Currently, I am a research assistant at the National University of Singapore (NUS). My recent work, presented at AAAI-25, investigates optimal contract design for collaborative machine learning, applying contract theory to design incentive mechanisms where models, rather than money, serve as rewards. This research contributes to the broader understanding of incentive structures in AI-driven markets, a topic becoming increasingly critical as society demands greater fairness, transparency, and democratization in AI development. This experience has reinforced my conviction that traditional economic frameworks, such as contract theory, must evolve beyond their descriptive origins to effectively address prescriptive challenges posed by emerging AI-driven markets.

Prior to coming to NUS and working on mechanism design, I earned an MPhil in Economics with distinction from Oxford, where my research focused on time series econometrics. The rigorous training cultivated my long-term interest in causal inference and honed my proficiency in statistical languages, such as R. I recognise that empirical questions in social science often involve small-data contexts, where robust theoretical frameworks, careful modelling, and data integrity significantly impact research validity. Emerging technologies such as large language models (LLMs) offer exciting possibilities for accelerating empirical research by enabling innovative methodologies and novel data forms. A central challenge—and an area I am eager to explore in my own research—is developing rigorous theoretical foundations to interpret and validate the outputs of these powerful yet opaque models. Specifically, the impressive linguistic capabilities and extensive embedded knowledge within LLMs create opportunities to innovate empirical methodologies and unlock previously inaccessible insights in economic studies.

Paid with Models: Optimal Contract Design for Collaborative Machine Learning

Bingchen Wang*, Zhaoxuan Wu, Fusheng Liu, and Bryan Kian Hsiang Low
The 39th AAAI Conference on Artificial Intelligence , AAAI-25, Philadelphia, PA, USA, 2025, forthcoming.
[4.6% Acceptance Rate—Oral Presentation]

Collaborative machine learning (CML) provides a promising paradigm for democratizing advanced technologies by enabling cost-sharing among participants. However, the potential for rent-seeking behaviors among parties can undermine such collaborations. Contract theory presents a viable solution by rewarding participants with models of varying accuracy based on their contributions. However, unlike monetary compensation, using models as rewards introduces unique challenges, particularly due to the stochastic nature of these rewards when contribution costs are privately held information. This paper formalizes the optimal contracting problem within CML and proposes a transformation that simplifies the non-convex optimization problem into one that can be solved through convex optimization algorithms. We conduct a detailed analysis of the properties that an optimal contract must satisfy when models serve as the rewards, and we explore the potential benefits and welfare implications of these contract-driven CML schemes through numerical experiments.
